Bikini Atoll is a coral reef in the Marshall Islands, located in the central Pacific Ocean. It consists of 23 islands surrounding a 229.4-square-mile (594.1 km²) central lagoon. The atoll lies at the northern end of the Ralik Chain, approximately 530 miles (850 km) northwest of the Marshall Islands' capital, Majuro. The largest island in the atoll is Bikini Island itself, followed by Eneu, Nam, and Enidrik.

The Marshallese people have inhabited Bikini Atoll for at least two thousand years, sustaining themselves through fishing, coconut and pandanus cultivation, and inter-atoll trade. Their traditional name for the atoll is "Pikinni," meaning "coconut place." Before 1946 the atoll's population was about 167 people who lived primarily on Bikini Island, organized around a chiefly system led by an iroij (paramount chief).

In February 1946, U.S. Navy Commodore Ben H. Wyatt visited Bikini and asked the islanders, through their leader King Juda, to relocate "temporarily" so the United States could begin "testing bombs for the good of mankind and to end all world wars." The Bikinians, devout Christians, agreed after deliberation. In March 1946 the entire population of 167 people was relocated to Rongerik Atoll, an uninhabited atoll about 125 miles east, that proved unable to support them due to limited fresh water and food. Within two years many were close to starvation.

Operation Crossroads, conducted in July 1946, comprised the first two of 23 nuclear tests the United States carried out at Bikini between 1946 and 1958. Test "Able," dropped from a B-29 bomber on July 1, 1946, and test "Baker," detonated underwater on July 25, 1946, were each about 23 kilotons. Baker produced an enormous radioactive water column and a base surge that contaminated the entire target fleet of nearly 90 retired warships, including the Japanese battleship Nagato, the U.S. carrier Saratoga, and the German cruiser Prinz Eugen. Many of these vessels still lie at the bottom of the lagoon today and form one of the most studied wreck dive sites in the world.

The most powerful test at Bikini was Castle Bravo, detonated on March 1, 1954. Castle Bravo was the first deliverable thermonuclear weapon test by the United States and yielded 15 megatons of TNT—roughly 1,000 times the energy of the Hiroshima bomb and more than twice what scientists had predicted. The unexpectedly large yield, combined with a wind shift, spread radioactive fallout over a much wider area than planned. Inhabitants of nearby Rongelap and Utirik atolls, as well as the crew of the Japanese tuna trawler Daigo Fukuryū Maru ("Lucky Dragon No. 5"), suffered serious radiation exposure. The fallout from Castle Bravo provoked an international outcry and helped catalyze the global anti-nuclear movement.

Between 1946 and 1958 the United States detonated a total of 23 nuclear devices at Bikini Atoll with a combined yield of roughly 42.2 megatons. The cumulative radioactive contamination rendered the atoll's soil and groundwater dangerous for long-term human habitation. Coconut, pandanus, and other staple foods absorb radioactive cesium-137 from the soil, making them unsafe to eat in significant quantities even today.

After the testing program ended, the U.S. government attempted in 1968 to declare Bikini safe and resettle the population. Several families returned in the early 1970s, but follow-up surveys in 1978 found dangerously elevated levels of cesium-137 and strontium-90 in their bodies, and they were evacuated again. The Bikinians have never been able to return to live permanently on their ancestral home.

In 1988 the Nuclear Claims Tribunal was established under the Compact of Free Association between the Republic of the Marshall Islands and the United States. The Tribunal awarded the Bikinians more than $563 million in compensation for personal injury, loss of land use, and cleanup costs—but the trust fund created to pay these claims was never fully funded by the U.S. Congress, and most of the awards remain unpaid.

Today the atoll is administered by the Bikini Atoll Local Government, which operates from Majuro and Kili Island, where most descendants of the original islanders now live. In 2010 UNESCO inscribed Bikini Atoll on its World Heritage List as a site that "bears witness to the dawn of the nuclear age" and represents both the destructive power of nuclear weapons and the displacement of indigenous peoples.

Despite the radioactive legacy, the lagoon's marine ecosystem has rebounded remarkably. The largely undisturbed coral reefs host populations of grey reef sharks, Napoleon wrasse, giant clams, and dense schools of jacks and barracuda. Scientific dive expeditions and a small, highly regulated dive tourism program operate periodically, drawn by the sunken Operation Crossroads fleet and the unique opportunity to observe a reef ecosystem nearly free of human impact.

Bikini Atoll remains a powerful symbol. The word "bikini" entered everyday language in 1946 when French designer Louis Réard named his new two-piece swimsuit after the atoll, anticipating that its appearance would be as explosive as the atomic tests. For the Marshallese people, however, Bikini is not a metaphor — it is a homeland that nuclear testing made uninhabitable, and a continuing struggle for justice, environmental restoration, and the right to return.
